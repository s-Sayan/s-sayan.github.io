Research

Within the realm of scientific exploration, I am immersed in the captivating domain of theoretical and observational cosmology. With a profound passion for unraveling the mysteries of the universe, my research endeavors are primarily focused on the intricate intricacies of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB), an extraordinary phenomenon that encompasses the oldest traceable light originating from the earliest stages of the cosmos.

The discovery of the CMB represents a remarkable achievement in the annals of scientific history. To comprehend its significance, we must delve into the depths of cosmic evolution. According to the prevailing cosmological framework, the universe emerged from a point of singularity known as the Big Bang, roughly 13.8 billion years ago. At the very early times, the universe was an incredibly hot, dense, and energetic state.

As the universe expanded and cooled down, approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang, a momentous event occurred. Photons, the elementary particles of light, became decoupled from the primordial plasma that permeated the cosmos. These liberated photons journeyed across the vast expanse of the expanding universe, permeating the fabric of space itself. Today, we can still detect the remnants of this ancient radiation, manifesting as the CMB.

The CMB is a faint cosmic glow that pervades the entire observable universe. It fills the vacuum of space, carrying imprints of the universe's early conditions and providing a tantalizing window into the primordial era. This relic radiation has undergone a dramatic redshift, stretching its original high-energy wavelengths into the microwave region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

To capture a snapshot of the CMB, scientists employ cutting-edge instruments and space-borne telescopes, meticulously mapping the sky with extraordinary precision. These observations enable us to acquire detailed measurements of the temperature fluctuations and polarization patterns within the CMB, which convey valuable insights into the structure, composition, and evolution of the universe.

Analyzing the subtle variations in the CMB allows cosmologists to probe fundamental questions about the cosmos, such as the age, geometry, and composition of the universe. It also sheds light on the formation and distribution of cosmic structures, such as galaxies, galaxy clusters, and dark matter.

By scrutinizing the CMB data in conjunction with theoretical models and computational simulations, we aim to refine our understanding of the early universe and its subsequent evolution. This intricate interplay between theory and observation allows us to construct more accurate cosmological frameworks and address fundamental enigmas, such as the nature of dark energy, the origin of cosmic inflation, and the ultimate fate of the universe.

In summary, my research endeavors are centered around the captivating field of theoretical and observational cosmology, with a specific focus on the exploration of the Cosmic Microwave Background. This extraordinary relic radiation, stemming from the Big Bang, provides us with a glimpse into the universe's primordial past and serves as a powerful tool for unraveling the profound mysteries that encompass the cosmos.

Publications

  • Cluster profiles from beyond-the-QE CMB lensing mass maps
    S. Saha, Louis Legrand, and Julien Carron
    The abundances of the galaxy clusters tell us about the expansion history of the universe. To know the mass calibration, we need to measure the masses of these dark matter halos. We use the lensing of the cosmic microwave background to probe the mass profile of these clusters. The convergence profile of such galaxy clusters is directly connected to the mass of the clusters. We use the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator (Carron et al. 2017) to estimate the convergence. This improves the Signal-to-Noise Ratio of mass measurement by 13% for a CMB-S4 like experiment. We provide theoretical forecasts and also apply our estimator on simulations.

  • Bayesian estimation of our local motion from the Planck-2018 CMB temperature map
    S. Saha, S. Shaikh, S. Mukherjee, T. Souradeep, and B. D. Wandelt
    The motion of our reference frame can be inferred from the CMB Dipole in mK level. But if there is some contribution from the intrinsic dipole, we won't be able to discriminate between these two. So we infer the velocity from the neighboring mode correlation in the small-scale CMB. We jointly infer the 'a_{lm}' and the power spectrum in a Bayesian approach, where we explore the posterior distribution using a Hamiltonian sampler that we have developed.
